# Cross-Border Payment Regulations: 10 APAC Markets

> Research compiled for banking PPTX presentation, March 2026
> Covers regulatory frameworks, FX controls, reporting thresholds, foreign enterprise accounts, and cash pooling feasibility

---

## 1. Singapore - Monetary Authority of Singapore (MAS)

**FX Control Level**: Free

**Key Restrictions**:
- No foreign exchange controls on capital movements in or out of Singapore
- No restrictions on repatriation of profits, dividends, or capital
- Activity-based regulation under the Payment Services Act (PSA) 2019, amended 2025
- Cross-border money transfer services (Activity C under PSA) require Major Payment Institution (MPI) license
- Enhanced anti-scam measures and stablecoin frameworks introduced in 2025-2026

**Reporting Threshold**:
- Cash/Bearer Negotiable Instruments (CBNI) exceeding SGD 20,000 (or equivalent) must be declared when entering/leaving Singapore
- Suspicious Transaction Reports (STRs) mandatory for all regulated entities
- Cross-border wire transfers: originator/beneficiary information required per MAS Notice PSN01

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign companies can open corporate bank accounts freely
- No minimum capital requirements for account opening (bank-dependent)
- Multi-currency accounts widely available (SGD, USD, EUR, CNY, etc.)
- Enhanced KYC/CDD for non-resident entities

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Yes (Full)
- Singapore is the premier APAC cash pooling hub alongside Hong Kong
- Both physical and notional cash pooling fully supported
- No regulatory barriers to cross-border cash pooling
- Major banks (DBS, OCBC, UOB, HSBC, Citi) offer integrated cross-border liquidity solutions
- Multi-currency notional pooling available

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- MAS Notice 626 (AML/CFT) applies to all financial institutions
- Corporate cheque phase-out by end of 2026
- Singapore participates in Project Nexus for real-time cross-border payments
- Transfer pricing guidelines (8th edition) apply to intercompany cash pooling arrangements

## 2. Hong Kong - Hong Kong Monetary Authority (HKMA)

**FX Control Level**: Free

**Key Restrictions**:
- No foreign exchange controls; free movement of capital
- Linked Exchange Rate System (LERS): HKD pegged to USD at 7.75-7.85 band
- No restrictions on cross-border payments, remittances, or profit repatriation
- Anti-Money Laundering and Counter-Terrorist Financing Ordinance (AMLO) governs compliance
- New HKD Stablecoin Ordinance requires HKMA licensing for fiat-pegged stablecoin issuers

**Reporting Threshold**:
- Cross-border physical currency: no specific declaration threshold for general transfers
- Hong Kong-to-Mainland transfers via FPS/Payment Connect: HKD 10,000/day (HKD 200,000/year)
- STR reporting mandatory under AMLO
- Common Reporting Standard (CRS) requires banks to identify tax residency and report to IRD

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign companies can incorporate and open bank accounts
- Non-resident directors/shareholders face enhanced due diligence (AML/KYC)
- Multi-currency accounts available at all major banks
- Territorial tax system: only HK-sourced profits taxable (8.25%/16.5% two-tier)
- Commercial Data Interchange (CDI) facilitates SME data sharing with banks

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Yes (Full)
- Hong Kong is a leading APAC treasury center alongside Singapore
- Physical and notional cash pooling fully supported
- Cross-border cash pooling with Mainland China available via SAFE-approved schemes
- Major global banks offer regional treasury management from HK
- HSBC launched tokenised deposit service for real-time HKD/USD corporate cash management

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- No withholding tax on dividends, interest, or royalties paid to non-residents
- HKMA participates in cross-border QR payment linkages (FPS PayConnect)
- Group 1a cryptoasset exposures treated as traditional asset exposures per Nov 2025 update
- Strong rule of law and independent judiciary

## 3. Taiwan - Financial Supervisory Commission (FSC) / Central Bank (CBC)

**FX Control Level**: Semi-controlled

**Key Restrictions**:
- CBC officially states "effectively no foreign exchange restrictions" but practical limits exist
- Annual FX remittance limits: USD 5 million for companies, USD 5 million for individuals (aggregate)
- Single remittance by non-resident not exceeding USD 100,000 may proceed directly through authorized banks
- Amounts exceeding thresholds require CBC approval
- CBC actively manages TWD exchange rate (intervenes to maintain stability)
- Four common international payment methods: cash in advance, L/C, D/P, D/A

**Reporting Threshold**:
- Remittances exceeding USD 100,000 (non-residents) or aggregate annual limits require declaration/approval
- Large cash transactions reported per AML regulations
- VASP registration required since November 2024 for virtual asset service providers

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign enterprises can open NTD and foreign currency accounts
- Requires local registration (branch office or subsidiary)
- Foreign investors must register with TWSE for securities trading (Investor ID)
- Minimum deposit typically NTD 1,000 at most banks

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Partial
- Domestic cash pooling available
- Cross-border cash pooling subject to CBC FX controls and annual limits
- Intercompany lending subject to regulatory approval for larger amounts
- Not as flexible as Singapore/HK due to CBC oversight of capital flows

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- CBC intervenes in FX markets to manage TWD volatility
- Fintech regulation evolving: stablecoin framework under Legislative Yuan review (submitted June 2025)
- Transfer pricing rules apply to intercompany transactions
- AML/CFT framework aligned with FATF standards

## 4. Australia - AUSTRAC (Australian Transaction Reports and Analysis Centre)

**FX Control Level**: Free

**Key Restrictions**:
- No foreign exchange controls on capital movements
- Free repatriation of profits, dividends, and capital
- AML/CTF Act 2006 (major overhaul with new AML/CTF Rules effective August 2025)
- New rules extend AML obligations to lawyers, accountants, real estate agents, and other "tranche two" entities from July 2026
- Risk-based supervision approach with emphasis on "harm reduction"

**Reporting Threshold**:
- Threshold Transaction Reports (TTRs): AUD 10,000 or more in cash or equivalents
- International Funds Transfer Instructions (IFTIs): all cross-border electronic transfers must be reported
- Suspicious Matter Reports (SMRs): mandatory when suspicion arises (no threshold)
- Annual AML/CTF compliance report required
- Payment Times Reporting: entities with >AUD 100 million consolidated revenue must report

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign companies must register with ASIC (Australian Securities and Investments Commission)
- Can open corporate bank accounts with Australian banks
- Must hold Australian Financial Services Licence (AFSL) or be authorized by licensee for financial services
- Financial Claims Scheme covers up to AUD 250,000 per account holder per ADI

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Yes (Full)
- Physical and notional cash pooling fully supported
- No regulatory barriers to cross-border cash pooling
- Major banks (CBA, ANZ, Westpac, NAB) and global banks offer treasury solutions
- Multi-currency pooling available

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- AUSTRAC 2025 AML/CTF Rules significantly enhance reporting and transparency for cross-border value transfers
- Enhanced KYC/CDD requirements under new rules
- APRA (Australian Prudential Regulation Authority) oversees banking prudential standards
- Australia participates in Project Nexus for cross-border instant payments

## 5. New Zealand - Financial Markets Authority (FMA) / Reserve Bank (RBNZ)

**FX Control Level**: Free

**Key Restrictions**:
- No foreign exchange controls; open capital account
- Free movement of funds in and out of New Zealand
- AML/CFT Act 2009 (updated regulations 2024) governs compliance
- Financial Market Infrastructures Act 2021 regulates payment and settlement systems
- Three AML/CFT supervisors: RBNZ (banks, NBDTs, life insurers), FMA (financial service providers), DIA (other reporting entities)

**Reporting Threshold**:
- Occasional transaction threshold: NZD 10,000 for wire transfers (domestic or international)
- Prescribed Transaction Reports (PTRs) for transactions meeting certain criteria
- Suspicious Activity Reports (SARs) mandatory
- Fraud Intelligence Exchange (FIX) Ecosystem allows 13 approved banks to share SAR-related information

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign companies can register and open bank accounts
- Standard KYC/CDD requirements apply
- NZD and multi-currency accounts available
- No specific restrictions on foreign enterprise banking

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Yes (Full)
- No regulatory barriers to cash pooling
- Physical and notional pooling supported
- Smaller market compared to Singapore/HK but fully functional
- Cross-border pooling arrangements unrestricted

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- FMA Financial Conduct Report 2025/26 sets regulatory priorities
- New Zealand adopts FX Global Code (NZFMA)
- OECD Pillar Two rules enacted for MNEs with >EUR 750 million global turnover
- Relatively small financial center but strong regulatory framework

## 6. Malaysia - Bank Negara Malaysia (BNM)

**FX Control Level**: Semi-controlled

**Key Restrictions**:
- Foreign Exchange Policy (FEP) Notices govern all FX transactions under Financial Services Act 2013 (FSA) and Islamic Financial Services Act 2013 (IFSA)
- Ringgit (MYR) is non-internationalized: cannot be freely traded offshore
- All domestic trade settlements must be in MYR
- Non-residents free to obtain foreign currency borrowing from licensed onshore banks
- Qualified Resident Investor (QRI) programme launched July 2025 to support two-way fund flows
- Schedule 14 transactions (FSA/IFSA) require BNM written approval

**Reporting Threshold**:
- Cross-border transactions require reporting through authorized dealers
- Physical currency: declaration required for amounts exceeding MYR equivalent thresholds
- All FX transactions must be conducted through licensed entities
- BNM actively monitors and penalizes non-compliance (e.g., RM 1 million fine on KAF Investment Bank, Feb 2026)

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Non-residents can open MYR and foreign currency accounts with licensed banks
- Foreign currency accounts (FCA) available for trade settlement
- Restrictions on MYR accounts for non-residents (limited purposes)
- Malaysia is world's largest Islamic finance center (16 fully-fledged Islamic banks)

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Partial
- Domestic cash pooling available
- Cross-border cash pooling restricted due to MYR non-internationalization
- Intercompany lending across borders requires BNM approval
- Physical pooling more feasible than notional pooling for cross-border
- BNM participating in Project Rialto (BIS Innovation Hub) for cross-border payment innovation

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- BNM reduced Overnight Policy Rate to 2.75% in July 2025
- Strict enforcement of FEP rules (penalties for non-compliance)
- Money Services Business (MSB) licensees subject to minimum capital fund requirements
- Malaysia participates in Project Nexus for cross-border instant payments
- Transfer pricing guidelines updated December 2024

## 7. Thailand - Bank of Thailand (BOT)

**FX Control Level**: Semi-controlled (liberalizing)

**Key Restrictions**:
- Exchange Control Act B.E. 2485 and Ministerial Regulation No. 13 govern FX
- All FX transactions must be conducted through authorized persons/banks
- BOT issued major FX relaxation regulations on 1 September 2025:
  - Annual outflow limit raised from USD 50,000 to USD 200,000
  - Reduced restrictions on negative lists
  - Modified FX management rules under national pooling method
- Proposed further relaxation (public hearing Dec 2025): increase offshore fund threshold to USD 10 million
- Physical currency: USD 20,000 or equivalent must be declared to customs when entering/leaving

**Reporting Threshold**:
- All FX transactions reported through authorized banks
- Physical currency declaration: USD 20,000 or equivalent
- Payment Systems Act requires safeguards against fraud, ML, and TF
- Transfers exceeding specific thresholds subject to enhanced scrutiny

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign companies can open THB and foreign currency accounts
- Foreign Business Act restrictions on certain business activities
- Non-resident baht accounts (NRBA) available with conditions
- BOT approval may be required for certain account types

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Partial (improving)
- Domestic cash pooling available
- Cross-border cash pooling subject to BOT FX regulations
- "National pooling method" rules being modified under 2025 relaxation
- BOT actively liberalizing FX ecosystem to improve treasury management
- Cross-border QR payment linkage with China launched October 2025 (Alipay, UnionPay, WeChat Pay)

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- BOT mega project to develop Thailand's FX ecosystem ongoing
- Significant FX liberalization trend since 2025
- Repatriation requirements being reshaped (Dec 2025 public hearing)
- Thailand participates in regional payment linkages (QR cross-border with China, ASEAN)

## 8. Indonesia - Bank Indonesia (BI) / Otoritas Jasa Keuangan (OJK)

**FX Control Level**: Semi-controlled

**Key Restrictions**:
- Generally no foreign currency controls on movement of funds, but significant reporting and documentation requirements
- All domestic transactions must be settled in Indonesian Rupiah (IDR) — mandatory since BI Regulation 17/3/PBI/2015
- FX purchases against Rupiah above USD 100,000 (or equivalent) per month per transactor require underlying transaction documents (BI Reg 24/10/PADG/2022)
- FX transactions between banks and foreign parties regulated under BI Regulation 18/19/PBI/2016
- Major payment system overhaul: BI Regulation No. 10/2025 (effective March 2026) creates single framework
- OJK oversees financial services; BI oversees payment systems and monetary policy

**Reporting Threshold**:
- FX activity reporting mandatory under BI Regulation 21/2/PBI/2019 for all resident-nonresident FX transactions
- FX purchase against IDR >USD 100,000/month requires underlying documents
- Cash export limits apply (declaration required for large amounts)
- All payment service providers must register under new BI Reg 10/2025

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign companies must establish legal entity (PT PMA - foreign investment company) to operate
- Can open IDR and foreign currency accounts
- IDR accounts mandatory for domestic transactions
- Foreign currency accounts available for trade-related purposes
- OJK fines exceeding IDR 100 million for financial statement violations

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Restricted
- Domestic cash pooling available but subject to BI regulations
- Cross-border cash pooling significantly restricted due to IDR mandatory settlement rules
- Intercompany lending across borders requires documentation and reporting
- Notional pooling limited by regulatory framework
- Indonesia Payment Systems Blueprint 2025-2030 aims to modernize but FX controls remain

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- Mandatory Rupiah usage for all domestic transactions (penalties for non-compliance)
- BI Regulation No. 4/2025 strengthens payment system governance
- Digital Rupiah initiative (programmability, composability, tokenization) under development
- Law No. 4/2023 (P2SK Law) reformed financial sector supervision
- Transfer pricing rules apply to intercompany arrangements

## 9. Vietnam - State Bank of Vietnam (SBV)

**FX Control Level**: Strictly controlled

**Key Restrictions**:
- VND is not freely convertible; SBV manages exchange rate through central rate mechanism
- All domestic transactions must be settled in VND
- Foreign exchange bureaus can only buy (not sell) cash foreign currency from individuals
- Only bureaus at international border crossings can sell FX to departing foreign passport holders
- Overseas lending by credit institutions requires SBV registration for terms >180 days (Circular 79/2025/TT-NHNN)
- SBV actively manages interbank exchange rate (~26,044 VND/USD as of Feb 2026)

**Reporting Threshold**:
- All FX transactions must go through authorized banks
- SBV inspects foreign exchange dealers for compliance
- Large transaction reporting per AML regulations
- Cross-border e-commerce platforms subject to new Electronic Commerce Law (extraterritorial application)

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign-invested enterprises (FIEs) can open VND and foreign currency accounts
- Direct Investment Capital Account (DICA) required for foreign investment capital flows
- Indirect Investment Capital Account for portfolio investments
- Strict separation of capital accounts and operating accounts
- Foreign companies cannot distribute directly to retail in certain sectors (e.g., pharmaceuticals)

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Restricted
- Domestic cash pooling limited and subject to SBV approval
- Cross-border cash pooling highly restricted due to strict FX controls
- Intercompany cross-border lending requires SBV registration
- VND non-convertibility is the primary barrier
- QR Pay and digital payment infrastructure growing but FX controls remain tight

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- Vietnam's Digital Technology Industry Law 2025 expanding regulatory framework
- Deposit Insurance Law 2025 (effective May 2026) updates depositor protection
- SBV monetary policy: proactive and flexible in volatile global environment
- Credit growth target managed by SBV (1.4% growth in first 2 months of 2026)
- New securities trading rules for foreign investors (March 2026)

## 10. Philippines - Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas (BSP)

**FX Control Level**: Semi-controlled

**Key Restrictions**:
- Liberalized FX regime but with documentation and registration requirements
- BSP does not require import registration but banks must report FX purchases to BSP before payment
- Outward investments over USD 60 million require prior BSP notification
- Strict documentation and KYC requirements for FX purchases
- FX derivatives: hedging encouraged but speculation restricted (BSP Circular 2025)
- Total FX hedging amount must not exceed actual underlying FX exposure
- Physical currency: amounts exceeding USD 10,000 must be declared to customs

**Reporting Threshold**:
- International Transactions Reporting System (ITRS) — all FX transactions reported to BSP
- Physical currency declaration: USD 10,000 or equivalent
- Enhanced Due Diligence (EDD) threshold for cash withdrawals: PHP 1 million (raised from PHP 500,000 per Circular 1230, Feb 2026)
- All cross-border transfers monitored by BSP

**Foreign Enterprise Accounts**:
- Foreign investors should register investments with BSP for repatriation privileges
- Peso time deposits with authorized agent banks (AAB) with ≥90-day maturity qualify
- Foreign companies can open peso and foreign currency accounts
- National Payment Systems Act (RA 11127, 2018) requires Operators of Payment Systems to register with BSP

**Cash Pooling Feasibility**: Partial
- Domestic cash pooling available
- Cross-border cash pooling subject to BSP FX regulations and ITRS reporting
- Registration of foreign investment with BSP recommended for smooth repatriation
- Intercompany lending across borders requires documentation
- Philippines participates in Project Nexus for cross-border instant payments

**Key Compliance Notes**:
- BSP closely monitors cross-border transfers (all commercial, universal, and thrift banks)
- Internet Transactions Act (ITA) applies to B2B and B2C internet transactions
- BSP Circular 1230 (Feb 2026) doubled EDD threshold for cash withdrawals
- Philippines has active fintech ecosystem under BSP oversight
- AMLA (Anti-Money Laundering Act) compliance mandatory

## Summary: Cash Pooling Hub Recommendations

### Tier 1 — Full Cash Pooling (Recommended Regional Hubs)
- **Singapore**: Premier APAC treasury center. No FX controls, full physical/notional pooling, multi-currency support, strong legal framework.
- **Hong Kong**: Equal to Singapore for treasury. Free capital flows, linked exchange rate stability, gateway to China via SAFE schemes.

### Tier 2 — Full Cash Pooling (Developed Markets)
- **Australia**: No barriers, strong regulatory framework, but geographically distant from ASEAN operations.
- **New Zealand**: Fully functional but smaller market; suitable for ANZ-focused operations.

### Tier 3 — Partial Cash Pooling (Requires Structuring)
- **Taiwan**: Feasible with planning around CBC annual limits. Good for Greater China operations.
- **Malaysia**: Domestic pooling works; cross-border limited by MYR controls. BNM liberalizing gradually.
- **Thailand**: Rapidly improving. BOT 2025 relaxations significantly expand possibilities. Watch for further liberalization.
- **Philippines**: Workable with proper BSP registration and ITRS compliance.

### Tier 4 — Restricted (Significant Barriers)
- **Indonesia**: Mandatory IDR settlement and documentation requirements make cross-border pooling difficult.
- **Vietnam**: Strict FX controls and VND non-convertibility are major barriers. Requires DICA structure.

## Key Regulatory Trends (2025-2026)

1. **FX Liberalization**: Thailand (BOT mega project), Malaysia (QRI programme), Indonesia (Blueprint 2025-2030)
2. **Real-Time Cross-Border Payments**: Project Nexus (India, Malaysia, Philippines, Singapore, Thailand), Project Rialto (BIS + BNM + MAS)
3. **Enhanced AML/CTF**: Australia (new rules Aug 2025, tranche two Jul 2026), Singapore (anti-scam measures), Philippines (ITRS enhancement)
4. **Digital Currency**: Indonesia (Digital Rupiah), Hong Kong (HKD stablecoin ordinance), Singapore (stablecoin framework)
5. **Cross-Border QR Payments**: Thailand-China linkage (Oct 2025), ASEAN QR payment network expansion
